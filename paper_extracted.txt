Abstract—Recently, Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) algorithms
have gained traction in image/video quality assessment
(IQA/VQA). These methods use virtual losses, like group contrastive and rank loss, as auxiliary tasks to adapt batch
normalization parameters, helping models generalize better to
distribution shifts between training and testing datasets. Group
contrastive loss clusters images into low- and high-quality groups
based on predicted quality scores, maximizing feature space
distance between them. However, its effectiveness relies on the
base model’s ability to accurately predict quality scores, which
can be compromised when distribution shifts occur, leading to
suboptimal adaptation and degraded performance. We propose
a novel clustering approach based on the assumption that highquality images contain richer high-level information, which is
extracted using a pre-trained VGG-16 model. Images are clustered by comparing the VGG-16 features of the highest quality
image in a batch with the others, enabling effective grouping
based on feature affinities. These accurate clusters enhance
the computation of contrastive loss, improving the adaptation
of batch normalization layers. Additionally, we introduce an
adaptive rank loss to reduce the impact of rank loss when the
base model can distinguish images with varying distortion levels.
Experimental results across multiple image quality assessment
datasets, including LIVE, CID-2013, KONIQ-10K, and SPAQ, as
well as algorithms like MetaIQA, HyperIQA, TReS, and MUSIQ,
show that the proposed method consistently performs better than
the existing Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) approach.
Index Terms—Test-time adaptation, Image quality assessment,
Batch normalization layer, Distribution shift, Group contrastive
loss.

I. I NTRODUCTION
Image Quality Assessment (IQA) is crucial for objectively
predicting an image’s perceptual quality, aligning with the
human visual system’s perception. It is also used as a loss
function in deep learning models for various image processing
and computer vision tasks. Methods such as those proposed
by [1]–[3] use deep features or semantic cues to assess image
quality, while others rely on low-level features like local
distortions [4], [5]. Despite these advances, the performance
of most IQA models is limited when evaluating images which
have a distribution different from the training dataset.

Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) has emerged as an effective
approach to handle distribution shifts between training and
testing datasets. TTA methods adjust a limited set of model parameters in an unsupervised manner during the testing phase to
adapt the baseline model to specific datasets. In literature a few
methods are proposed for TTA such as augmentation-based
TTA [6], pseudo-labeling [7], [8], and entropy minimization
[9] have demonstrated significant potential in enhancing model
performance across various tasks. Since labels for testing data
are unavailable during TTA, auxiliary tasks are employed
to facilitate adaptation. These include aligning feature similarities within a group [10] or across previous test samples
[7], generating pseudo-class labels [11], creating augmented
views [12], [13], and applying data augmentations such as
color transformations [14]. These strategies help compute
the pseudo-contrastive loss to adapt the batch normalization
layers. At the same time, TTA methods explicitly designed
for Image Quality Assessment (IQA) tasks are very limited,
and the TTA strategies mentioned above cannot be directly
applied to IQA. Recently, Roy et al. [10] introduced a TTA
method for no-reference IQA that utilizes pseudo auxiliary
tasks such as group contrastive (GC) and rank loss to adjust
batch normalization layers. To estimate the GC loss, the feature distance between clusters of low-quality and high-quality
images is calculated. These clusters are formed based on the
quality score predictions by the base model. This IQA-TTA
method faces challenges when the training and testing data
distribution differs significantly. In such cases, the base model
may struggle to generate accurate image clusters, leading to
erroneous GC loss estimation and limited performance. With
this in mind, there is a need for a more effective clustering
algorithm for IQA-TTA and a new IQA-TTA algorithm.
This paper introduces a novel TTA approach for noreference IQA that utilizes pre-trained deep learning models,
specifically VGG-16, to improve perceptual feature grouping. The proposed method incorporates two auxiliary loss
functions—feature affinity-based group contrastive loss and
adaptive rank loss—to enable adaptation during inference. The

II. M OTIVATION
The IQA-TTA approach [10] utilizes group contrastive and
rank losses to adapt the parameters of batch normalization
layers, relying on the base model’s ability to classify images
into high- and low-quality clusters. However, this method
assumes that the base model can accurately predict the relative
quality scores for unseen data and subsequently create the
clusters of low- and high-quality images to compute the
GC loss. This assumption fails when there is a significant
distribution shift between the training and testing datasets.

This section presents a novel TTA algorithm for noreference IQA. The proposed method incorporates two auxiliary tasks: feature affinity-based perceptual group contrastive
loss and adaptive rank loss, to adapt the feature extractor of
a pre-trained IQA model during inference. These tasks aim
to align the training and testing data distributions without
requiring labeled data, enabling effective quality assessment
even under distributional shifts. The details of the proposed
algorithm are given in the following subsections:
A. Problem statement
Following the framework of IQA-TTA [10], we consider
a source/base model, denoted as Sθ comprising a feature
extractor (θf ) and a quality regressor (θr ). TTA focuses
on updating only the feature extractor θf during inference,
aligning it with the test data distribution while preserving
the characteristics of the training distribution. To facilitate
this update, a projection head θP is added with the feature
extractor (θf ) to project extracted features into a lowerdimensional space. The following loss function is utilized to
jointly optimize θf and θP , leveraging auxiliary losses in the
absence of true labels.

B. Feature Affinity-based Group Contrastive (FAGC) loss
The overall pipeline of the Feature Affinity-based Group
Contrastive (FAGC) loss is illustrated in Fig. 2. This proposed
loss enhances the separability between high- and low-quality
image clusters. In the IQA-TTA method, a mini-batch of
images is divided into two clusters: C1 , representing lowquality images, and C2 , representing high-quality images,
based on their predicted scores from the base model. The
group contrastive (GC) loss is then calculated by maximizing
the feature distance between these two clusters.
Notably, the IQA-TTA [10] model forms clusters based on
predictions made by the base model, which may not perform
efficiently on the testing data, resulting in improperly formed
clusters. At the same time, it can be assumed that images
with richer high-level information possess better perceptual
quality. Motivated by this observation, we propose a new
clustering method for GC loss calculation leveraging the pretrained VGG-16 architecture. For a given set of images,

IV. E XPERIMENTS AND R ESULTS
This section presents the implementation details of the
proposed method, followed by a comprehensive performance
evaluation and a comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
Furthermore, an ablation study is conducted to analyze the individual contributions of the proposed FAGC and adaptive rank
losses. The proposed algorithm is also exclusively compared
with IQA-TTA [10], as it is, to the best of our knowledge, the
most recent method in the literature that applies Test-Time
Adaptation (TTA) for image quality assessment metrics.
A. Implementation details
The proposed model is implemented on an NVIDIA A100
GPU with 80 GB of RAM, utilizing the PyTorch framework
on a Linux-based operating system. A batch size of 8 is used
to update the batch normalization layers, while the rest of the
base model parameters/hyperparameters remain unaltered. The
proposed algorithm is applied to perform TTA for four IQA
algorithms (details provided in Table II) and is evaluated on
four datasets (details provided in Table I) across these IQA
algorithms. For MetaIQA, the base model without adaptation
processes a batch of eight images in 5.603 ms, IQA-TTA [10]
takes approximately 6.846 ms, and our proposed method takes
around 7.204 ms.

C. Ablation study
An ablation study in Tables IV and V assesses the impact of
feature extraction methods (such as pre-trained deep-learning
methods) and similarity metrics. Among feature extraction
methods, pretrained VGG-16 outperformed others on the LIVE
[16], CID [17], and KONIQ-10K [18] datasets in terms of
PLCC and SRCC. Cosine similarity in the latent space proved
to be the most effective feature affinity metric, as shown in Table V. Additionally, the scatter plots in Fig. 4 demonstrate that
the predicted scores from the proposed algorithm align more
closely with the regression line, highlighting its effectiveness.
V. C ONCLUSION
In this work, we proposed a new TTA algorithm for IQA
tasks, incorporating two auxiliary tasks: feature affinity-based
group contrastive loss (FAGC) and adaptive rank loss. The
FAGC loss, based on pre-trained deep learning models, efficiently clusters low and high-quality images. Additionally,
adaptive rank loss mitigates the impact of rank loss when the
base model distinguishes images with varying distortion levels.
The proposed algorithm is tested with multiple algorithms

across several datasets, outperforming existing methods in
TTA tasks. One limitation of the proposed work is that it does
not perform satisfactorily when the base model is unable to
accurately identify images with the best perceptual quality. An
